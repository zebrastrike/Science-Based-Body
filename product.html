PEPTIDE FAQ

Research-focused questions and answers.

Clear, compliance-aware answers intended for research education, not medical guidance.

Research peptides are short chains of amino acids used in laboratory and preclinical research. They are not approved drugs and are supplied for research and educational use only.

Most are produced using solid-phase peptide synthesis, then purified and tested with analytical methods such as HPLC and mass spectrometry. Methods vary by manufacturer and compound.

Purity is typically reported as the percentage of the target compound measured by analytical testing. It describes lab measurements, not clinical outcomes.

A COA is a lab document that summarizes identity and purity testing, often including chromatograms or mass spec data, batch references, and measured purity.

Storage guidance varies by compound and should follow the lab documentation. Common guidance is cool, dry, and light-protected storage, but always reference the COA or dossier.

It means the material is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease and is not for human consumption or medical use.

Many peptides have limited or early-stage data in humans. Research findings should be interpreted carefully and within the context of peer-reviewed evidence.

Look for retention time, peak profile, and the reported purity percentage, then compare with the lab methodology. If needed, consult the testing laboratory for clarification.
